## Idea: Scouts Run a Pop-Up Night Market for the Community

### Description
The troop organises and runs a one-evening pop-up night market in a local park, community hall, or car park, where each patrol designs and operates its own stall. Every stall must use a genuine scouting skill as its core offering. One patrol might run a rope bracelet workshop where visitors pay a small fee to learn paracord knots. Another could operate a campfire cooking stall selling s'mores or damper bread cooked on a portable fire pit. A third might offer a wilderness challenge game with orienteering puzzles or fire-steel spark challenges. Another could run a stargazing station with a borrowed telescope and constellation guides they have made.

The project uses two weekly meetings plus the event night itself, keeping the timeline tight and energy high. The first meeting is a hands-on "build and test" session. Leaders give a five-minute briefing: each patrol picks a scouting skill, builds a prototype stall, and tries it out on the other patrols. This is not a formal pitch presentation — patrols physically set up, run a trial version of their stall for the rest of the troop to visit, and get live feedback by seeing what works and what falls flat. This cross-patrol testing means every scout interacts with every other patrol's idea, and the session is active and noisy rather than sitting-and-listening. Leaders circulate to help patrols refine their concept and handle practical details like budgeting and any safety considerations. The second meeting is final preparation: patrols improve their stalls based on what they learned, make signage, practise demonstrations, and do a full dress rehearsal where patrols rotate through each other's stalls again. The third session is the event night itself.

All proceeds go to a cause the troop votes on together, whether that is funding their next camp, buying new equipment, or donating to a local charity. After the event closes, the troop gathers for a quick ten-minute wrap-up on the night itself — no separate debrief meeting — where each patrol shares one highlight and one thing they would change.

What makes this idea distinctive is that the scouts are not just participants in an activity planned for them. They are entrepreneurs, teachers, and hosts. They practise scouting skills not by doing exercises but by having to teach those skills to the public, which demands a much deeper level of understanding. The age range works naturally because older scouts (13-14) take on customer-facing and coordination roles while younger scouts (10-11) handle hands-on tasks like crafting products or running games, and everyone has a visible, meaningful contribution. The cross-patrol testing sessions in meetings one and two ensure the troop bonds as a whole, not just within patrol silos — every scout sees and contributes feedback to every stall.

### Advantages
- Scouts learn real-world entrepreneurial and organisational skills: budgeting, customer service, and project management through doing, not paperwork
- Teaching scouting skills to the public forces deeper mastery than simply practising them in a meeting
- Only two prep meetings plus the event night keeps energy high and avoids planning fatigue — the ratio of doing to admin is heavily weighted toward doing
- Cross-patrol testing in both prep sessions builds troop-wide bonds and gives every scout hands-on exposure to every stall concept
- Naturally inclusive across the full 10-14 age range, with scalable responsibilities for different maturity levels
- Creates a visible, positive presence for the troop in the local community, which can help with recruitment and public goodwill
- Covers multiple badge requirements organically across cooking, crafts, leadership, teamwork, and community service
- The shared fundraising goal unifies patrols around a common purpose, while friendly stall variety keeps things interesting without adversarial competition
- Produces a tangible outcome (money raised, community connections made) that scouts can feel proud of long after the event
- The concept is easy to explain in a single sentence: "Each patrol runs a stall teaching a scouting skill to the public, and we raise money for a cause we choose together"
