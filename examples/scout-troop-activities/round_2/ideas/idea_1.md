## Idea: "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Immersive Survival Campaign

### Description
The troop leader runs a connected survival narrative spanning 4-5 weekly meetings that culminates in a weekend camping trip. The premise: the scouts are members of an expedition team whose supply ship has been lost. Each week they must master a real survival or scouting skill to progress through the story, with a simple shared map that the troop fills in together as they advance.

**Simplified structure.** The campaign has been streamlined to remove unnecessary complexity. There is no multi-token economy or branching consequence system. Instead, each weekly session has a single clear mission with a pass/fail outcome for each patrol, and completed missions earn that patrol a star on the shared map. The map is the only tracking mechanism -- it is visible, tactile, and requires zero bookkeeping from scouts. At the culminating campout, patrols that earned more stars get a small head start (15 minutes) on the final challenge, but all patrols do the same activities with the same equipment. This eliminates the risk of material inequality at the campout while still rewarding consistent effort.

**Each weekly session teaches a genuine skill framed as a story challenge.** Week 1 involves orienteering and compass work ("You have washed ashore -- use these bearings to locate the supply cache"). Week 2 is a shelter-building challenge using lashing and tarp techniques. Week 3 is a first aid scenario ("A team member is injured -- stabilise them and get them to safety"). Week 4 is a cooking challenge using limited, odd ingredients. Week 5 (optional, if the schedule allows) is a nighttime navigation exercise. The leader picks the skills they want to teach and slots them into the narrative -- the story is a thin wrapper, not a complex branching plot. Each session stands alone well enough that a scout who misses a week can rejoin without confusion; they simply were "scouting ahead" in the story.

**The narrative layer is kept deliberately light.** Rather than sealed mission envelopes, fictional journal entries, and elaborate fiction, the leader opens each session with a two-minute verbal briefing in character: "Expedition team, here is our situation this week..." and then the scouts get straight into the hands-on activity. The story exists to connect the sessions and give context to the skills, not to be an elaborate production. This removes the dependency on the leader being a skilled storyteller -- any leader who can read a short briefing aloud can run this. The fiction is a seasoning, not the main course.

**Age-range differentiation is built into the patrol structure.** Within each mission, the leader provides tiered challenge levels. For orienteering, younger scouts work with simpler bearings and shorter routes while older scouts navigate more complex multi-leg courses. For shelter building, the baseline is a basic tarp shelter, but older scouts are challenged to build one that meets additional criteria (elevated, wind-resistant, fits the whole patrol). This is not additional complexity for the leader -- it is simply having two versions of the instruction sheet per activity.

**Cross-patrol collaboration is woven into the campaign.** At least two of the weekly sessions include a troop-wide challenge component where patrols must cooperate. For example, in the first aid week, each patrol stabilises one "casualty" but then all patrols must coordinate to transport all casualties to a single evacuation point within a time limit. In the cooking week, each patrol prepares one course of a shared meal that the whole troop eats together. This prevents the campaign from fragmenting the troop into rival factions and ensures scouts build relationships beyond their patrol.

**Quiet and younger scouts get structured leadership moments.** Each week, a different scout within each patrol is designated the "Expedition Specialist" for that session's skill. The Specialist reads the mission brief to the patrol, makes the key decisions during the activity, and reports back to the troop at the end. The leader assigns this role deliberately, rotating it to ensure every scout leads at least once during the campaign. This is a concrete structural mechanism, not a hope that leadership will distribute itself naturally.

**The campaign culminates in a weekend campout** where all the skills come together. Patrols navigate to a campsite using the map skills they have practised, build real shelters, cook a meal with constrained supplies, and complete a final challenge to "signal for rescue." Because the weekly sessions have been practising these exact skills, the campout is genuinely an integration exercise rather than a new challenge. Scouts experience the satisfaction of competence -- they know how to do these things because they have been doing them for a month.

**Practical requirements are minimal.** The leader needs a large poster-board map, a set of star stickers or markers, printed two-page mission briefs for each week, and standard camping and scouting equipment the troop likely already owns. Total additional cost is under ten pounds/dollars. Preparation time is roughly one hour per session, front-loaded -- the leader can plan the whole campaign in a single planning session of three to four hours.

### Advantages
- Transforms routine skill-building meetings into a connected journey that scouts look forward to each week, without requiring elaborate production values or storytelling talent
- Teaches real, badge-relevant scouting skills (orienteering, fire-building, first aid, cooking, lashing, knot-tying) in contexts that give them genuine purpose
- Simple tracking via a shared map with stars -- no token economies, no bookkeeping, no complex rules for scouts to remember
- A 4-5 week run with an optional extension is short enough to maintain momentum and recover from a weak session, unlike a gruelling 8-week commitment
- Tiered challenge levels within each activity ensure 10-year-olds and 14-year-olds are both appropriately stretched
- Cross-patrol collaboration sessions build troop-wide cohesion alongside patrol identity
- The rotating Expedition Specialist role guarantees every scout gets a concrete leadership moment
- Culminates in a memorable campout where scouts apply everything they have learned, creating genuine competence and achievement
- Low cost (under ten pounds/dollars beyond normal equipment) and modest preparation time (one planning session plus one hour per weekly meeting)
- Each session is self-contained enough that a scout who misses a week can rejoin without being lost
