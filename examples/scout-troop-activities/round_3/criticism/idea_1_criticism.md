## Evaluation: "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Survival Campaign with Discovery Moments

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Fun | 7 | The discovery moments and narrative framing add genuine excitement, but the week-to-week reality is standard skill drills with light dressing. |
| Simplicity | 6 | Gradual introduction helps, but the sheer number of interlocking mechanics (specialist roles, shared map, rubrics, extensions, cross-patrol phases) creates significant cognitive load for leaders to manage and scouts to internalise. |
| Challenge | 8 | Genuine survival skills are taught with clear criteria and self-selecting extensions, though the collective progress model softens individual accountability. |
| Social Factor | 7 | The collective progress mechanic and cross-patrol collaboration are structurally sound, but the claim that helping struggling patrols will happen organically is optimistic and untested. |
| **Aggregate** | **7.0** | |

### Critical Analysis

#### Fun (Score: 7)
The hidden cache discoveries and mid-activity twists are the strongest fun elements here, but they are each one-off moments within sessions that are otherwise conventional scouting activities. Finding a waterproof container once during orienteering is exciting; the other 45 minutes of compass work is still compass work. The idea leans heavily on the premise that a thin narrative wrapper ("your supply ship is lost") will sustain excitement across four to five weeks, but there is no evidence that 10-14 year olds will maintain investment in a storyline delivered via two-minute verbal briefings with no visual props, characters, or interactive fiction elements beyond the caches. By week three or four, the novelty of "there might be a hidden thing" has become an expected pattern rather than a surprise. The cooking challenge with constrained odd ingredients has genuine fun potential, but the first aid relay and shelter building are activities most scout troops already run -- the narrative skin does not fundamentally change the experience of bandaging a pretend casualty. The lack of any competitive element (deliberately removed in favour of collective progress) also removes a significant driver of excitement for this age group; many 10-14 year olds find inter-patrol competition thrilling, and this idea offers nothing to replace that energy. The campout culmination is genuinely exciting but is a single weekend payoff for a month of buildup.

#### Simplicity (Score: 6)
The idea claims simplicity through gradual introduction, but the underlying system is not simple -- it is complex with a phased rollout. Consider what a leader must manage simultaneously by week three: a narrative briefing, a hidden cache to plant in advance, a mid-activity twist to time correctly, printed rubrics to distribute and assess, a shared map to update, an Expedition Specialist role to assign and offer choices within, base and extension challenges to run in parallel, a cross-patrol collaboration phase to orchestrate, and catch-up briefings for any scouts who missed previous weeks. That is a substantial operational burden. From the scouts' perspective, the gradual introduction helps, but by mid-campaign they are navigating multiple layered systems. The idea also assumes the leader can explain the flexible Specialist role clearly enough that a 10-year-old understands they have a choice of sub-roles within their role -- this is abstract and likely to cause confusion. The printed rubrics are a simplicity win for assessment, but rubrics with multiple observable criteria need to be checked in real time across five patrols, which is logistically demanding for a single leader. The claim that each session is "self-contained enough" for a returning scout understates how disorienting it would be to rejoin a narrative campaign with accumulated context, cached items, and established group dynamics.

#### Challenge (Score: 8)
This is the strongest dimension. The idea teaches genuine, practically useful survival skills with clear success criteria that make mastery observable and concrete. The self-selecting extension model is well-designed for mixed-ability groups and avoids the social cost of tiered sorting. The rubrics ("shelter stays standing for five minutes, sheds water when tested with a bucket") set a meaningful bar that requires real effort to clear. However, the collective progress mechanic undermines individual challenge to some degree: if the threshold is "four out of five patrols must succeed," then one patrol can fail without consequence, and the troop still advances. This creates a free-rider problem where a patrol that is struggling knows the troop will likely advance anyway. The idea also does not address what happens if the troop repeatedly fails to meet the collective threshold -- repeating a "modified version" of the same challenge the following week could feel punitive and boring rather than challenging, especially for patrols that already succeeded. The skills themselves (orienteering, shelter building, first aid, cooking) are well-chosen and badge-relevant, but they are also entirely standard scouting curriculum -- the challenge here is in the execution and criteria, not in the skill selection.

#### Social Factor (Score: 7)
The collective progress mechanic is the centrepiece of the social design, and it is theoretically sound: patrols that finish early are incentivised to help others. In practice, this relies on an assumption about adolescent behaviour that is not well-supported. A patrol of 12-year-olds who have just completed a difficult shelter build are more likely to relax and socialise amongst themselves than proactively seek out a struggling patrol to assist. Without explicit leader direction ("Patrol 3, go help Patrol 5"), this helping behaviour is unlikely to emerge spontaneously, especially in the early weeks before troop cohesion has been established. The cross-patrol collaboration moments (first aid evacuation, shared cooking) are more structurally guaranteed to produce interaction, which is good, but these are specific moments within sessions, not the default state. The Expedition Specialist role provides a leadership moment for every scout, but leadership within your own patrol does not build cross-patrol friendships. The idea also removes inter-patrol competition entirely, which -- while reducing blame dynamics -- also removes a powerful bonding mechanism within patrols. Shared adversity against a rival patrol is one of the strongest friendship-building forces in scouting, and this idea offers collective progress against an abstract narrative goal as a substitute, which is a weaker social glue. The culminating campout is the strongest social element, but one weekend cannot compensate for four weeks of sessions where cross-patrol interaction is structurally present but not deeply embedded.
