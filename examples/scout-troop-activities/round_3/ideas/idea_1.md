## Idea: "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Survival Campaign with Discovery Moments

### Description
The troop leader runs a connected survival campaign spanning 4-5 weekly meetings that culminates in a weekend camping trip. The premise: the scouts are members of an expedition team whose supply ship has been lost. Each week they must master a real survival skill to progress, with a shared troop map tracking their collective journey.

**The narrative layer is light but includes genuine surprise elements.** The leader opens each session with a two-minute verbal briefing -- no elaborate storytelling talent required. However, each week includes one hidden discovery moment that the scouts encounter during the activity itself, not during a briefing. For example, during the orienteering exercise, one of the compass bearings leads to a concealed waterproof container holding the next clue about what happened to the supply ship and a practical item they will need later (a fire steel, a signal mirror, a water purification tablet). During shelter building, halfway through the exercise the leader announces deteriorating weather conditions and patrols must adapt their shelters within a time limit. These embedded surprises turn standard activities into genuinely unpredictable experiences without requiring the leader to be a performer -- they just need to hide a box or set a timer.

**Each weekly session teaches a genuine skill through an active challenge.** Week 1 involves orienteering and compass work with the hidden cache discovery. Week 2 is shelter building with the mid-exercise weather twist. Week 3 is a first aid scenario run as a timed relay where patrols race to stabilise casualties and then all patrols must coordinate a troop-wide evacuation. Week 4 is a cooking challenge using only the supplies they have "salvaged" throughout the campaign (constrained, odd ingredients). Week 5 (optional) is nighttime navigation. The leader picks the skills they want to teach and slots them into the narrative. Each session stands alone well enough that a scout who misses a week can rejoin without confusion; the leader gives them a one-minute catch-up briefing and, where a missed skill is needed later, pairs them with an experienced patrol-mate during the campout.

**The campaign uses collective progress, not inter-patrol competition.** Rather than patrols earning individual stars, the whole troop advances together on the shared map. Each session has a clear troop-wide objective -- for example, "at least four out of five patrols must complete the shelter to standard within the time limit for the expedition to advance." This means patrols that finish early are incentivised to help struggling patrols rather than gloat. If the troop fails a session objective, they repeat a modified version of that challenge the following week (the story frames this as needing to "find another route"). This removes the competitive tension between patrols, eliminates the risk of one scout being blamed for a patrol failure, and makes the map a source of collective pride rather than comparison.

**Age-range differentiation uses a choice model, not sorting.** Rather than the leader assigning scouts to tiers, each activity offers a base challenge and an "advanced extension" that any scout can attempt after completing the base. For orienteering, everyone does the core route; scouts who finish can attempt a bonus multi-leg course. For shelter building, everyone builds a basic tarp shelter; those who finish can attempt to make it elevated or add wind-resistance features. This is self-selecting and aspirational rather than sorted, which avoids the social friction of being told you are in the easier group. Younger scouts who want to try the extension are welcome to. The extension is visible to everyone as something to reach for, not a separate track.

**Cross-patrol collaboration is the default, not the exception.** At least three of the four or five weekly sessions include a troop-wide component. The first aid evacuation, the cooking of a shared meal, and the collective progress mechanic all require patrols to work across boundaries. Even in patrol-focused activities like shelter building, the "help other patrols finish so the troop advances" mechanic means scouts are regularly interacting beyond their patrol. The culminating campout is explicitly a whole-troop exercise -- patrols navigate to the campsite together, share resources, and the final "signal for rescue" challenge requires the entire troop to coordinate.

**Every scout contributes through a rotating specialist role, adapted to their comfort.** Each week, a different scout within each patrol is the "Expedition Specialist" for that session. The leader assigns this deliberately to ensure every scout leads at least once. Critically, the role is flexible in how it is performed -- the Specialist might read the mission brief aloud, or they might choose to delegate the reading and instead be the decision-maker during the activity, or they might be the one who reports back to the troop at the end. The leader offers the Specialist a choice of which aspect of the role to take on. This preserves the structural guarantee that every scout leads, while accommodating scouts who would be paralysed by mandatory public speaking.

**Clear success criteria replace vague pass/fail judgements.** Each session has a printed rubric with specific, observable criteria. Shelter building: "shelter stays standing for 5 minutes with no hands, fits all patrol members underneath, sheds water when tested with a bucket." First aid: "casualty correctly placed in recovery position, bleeding controlled with direct pressure, emergency services information relayed accurately." These criteria are shared with scouts at the start of each session so they know exactly what they are working toward. The leader does not make subjective judgements -- the shelter either stands or it does not.

**The campaign culminates in a weekend campout** where all the skills come together. The troop navigates to a campsite using practised map skills, builds real shelters, cooks a meal with constrained supplies, and completes a final troop-wide challenge to "signal for rescue" using the items discovered in hidden caches throughout the campaign. Because the weekly sessions have been practising these exact skills, the campout is genuinely an integration exercise. Scouts experience the satisfaction of competence -- they know how to do these things because they have been doing them for a month.

**Introduction is gradual, not front-loaded.** In week 1, the leader explains only three things: "We are running a survival campaign over the next month. Each week we learn a skill. It all comes together at the campout." The map, the specialist role, and the specific mechanics are introduced one at a time as they become relevant. By week 3, scouts understand the full picture through experience rather than explanation. Ten-year-olds never need to hold the whole structure in their heads at once.

**Practical requirements are minimal.** The leader needs a large poster-board map, printed one-page rubrics for each week, a few items to hide as cache discoveries (total value under five pounds/dollars), and standard camping equipment the troop likely already owns. Total additional cost is under fifteen pounds/dollars. Preparation time is roughly one hour per session, front-loaded -- the leader can plan the whole campaign in a single planning session of three to four hours.

### Advantages
- Transforms routine skill-building meetings into a connected journey with genuine surprise moments (hidden caches, mid-activity twists) that create excitement without requiring elaborate production or storytelling talent
- Teaches real, badge-relevant scouting skills (orienteering, shelter building, first aid, cooking, lashing) in contexts that give them genuine purpose
- Collective troop progress on the shared map encourages patrols to help each other rather than compete, building troop cohesion and eliminating the risk of one scout being blamed for a patrol failure
- Clear, printed success criteria for each activity ensure scouts know what they are working toward and leaders do not make subjective judgements
- Self-selecting advanced extensions let ambitious scouts stretch themselves without the social friction of being sorted into ability tiers
- Cross-patrol collaboration features in at least three of five sessions, making inter-patrol cooperation the norm rather than the exception
- The flexible Expedition Specialist role guarantees every scout a leadership moment while accommodating different comfort levels around public speaking
- Gradual introduction means scouts absorb the structure through experience -- no information overload in week one
- A 4-5 week run is short enough to maintain momentum, and the surprise elements in each session provide recovery from any weaker week
- Culminates in a memorable campout where scouts apply everything they have learned, using items they discovered throughout the campaign
- Low cost (under fifteen pounds/dollars) and modest preparation time (one planning session plus one hour per weekly meeting)
- Each session is self-contained enough that a scout who misses a week can rejoin, with explicit catch-up pairing for skill gaps
