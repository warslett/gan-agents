# Summary — Fun Scout Troop Activities for Mixed-Age Group

## 1. Original Problem

> "give me some fun ideas for a scout troop leader to organise for their scout troop. The troop has girls and boys aged 10 - 14"

[Research undertaken first](research/index.md)

**Scoring Dimensions:**
- **Fun** — How much excitement, laughter, and active engagement does this activity generate for 10-14 year olds?
- **Simplicity** — How simple will it be to explain to a room of 10-14 year olds?
- **Challenge** — Does this push scouts to learn new skills or develop existing ones in a meaningful way?
- **Social Factor** — How much does this encourage collaboration, trust, and stronger friendships within the troop?

## 2. Final Idea

### "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Survival Campaign with Discovery Moments

The troop leader runs a connected survival campaign spanning 4-5 weekly meetings that culminates in a weekend camping trip. The premise: the scouts are members of an expedition team whose supply ship has been lost. Each week they must master a real survival skill to progress, with a shared troop map tracking their collective journey.

The narrative layer is light but includes genuine surprise elements. The leader opens each session with a two-minute verbal briefing -- no elaborate storytelling talent required. However, each week includes one hidden discovery moment that the scouts encounter during the activity itself. For example, during the orienteering exercise, one of the compass bearings leads to a concealed waterproof container holding the next clue about what happened to the supply ship and a practical item they will need later (a fire steel, a signal mirror, a water purification tablet). During shelter building, halfway through the exercise the leader announces deteriorating weather conditions and patrols must adapt their shelters within a time limit. These embedded surprises turn standard activities into genuinely unpredictable experiences without requiring the leader to be a performer.

Each weekly session teaches a genuine skill through an active challenge: orienteering with a hidden cache discovery, shelter building with a mid-exercise weather twist, a first aid scenario run as a timed relay with troop-wide evacuation coordination, a cooking challenge using only "salvaged" supplies, and an optional nighttime navigation exercise. Each session stands alone well enough that a scout who misses a week can rejoin without confusion.

The campaign uses collective progress rather than inter-patrol competition. The whole troop advances together on the shared map, with session objectives like "at least four out of five patrols must complete the shelter to standard." Patrols that finish early are incentivised to help struggling patrols. Age-range differentiation uses a self-selecting choice model rather than assigned tiers -- every scout completes a base challenge, with advanced extensions available for anyone who finishes early. Cross-patrol collaboration features in at least three of five sessions. A rotating "Expedition Specialist" role guarantees every scout a leadership moment, with flexibility in how the role is performed to accommodate different comfort levels. Clear, printed rubrics with specific observable criteria replace subjective pass/fail judgements. The whole structure is introduced gradually, week by week, so ten-year-olds never need to hold the full picture in their heads at once. The campaign culminates in a weekend campout where all skills come together and scouts use items discovered in hidden caches throughout the campaign.

**Why it survived:** The Lost Expedition idea consistently scored highest across all three rounds, improving from an aggregate of 5.5 in Round 1 to 6.5 in Round 2 and 7.0 in Round 3. It earned the strongest Challenge scores (7, 8, 8) throughout the process and steadily improved on its initial weaknesses. Criticism about over-complex token systems led to simplified map-based tracking. Concerns about inter-patrol competition causing resentment drove a shift to collective progress. Feedback about narrative dependency on leader talent produced the "light narrative with surprise moments" approach. Issues with age-range sorting were addressed through self-selecting extensions. The idea's core strength -- teaching genuine scouting skills within a connected, purposeful framework -- proved more resilient to criticism than the alternatives, and each round of adversarial feedback produced concrete structural improvements.

## 3. Ideas Overview

3 ideas were generated in Round 1:

1. **"Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Immersive Survival Campaign Game** -- A 6-8 week narrative campaign where patrols master real survival skills framed as story challenges, tracking progress with resource tokens and culminating in a weekend campout.

2. **Scouts Run a Pop-Up Night Market for the Community** -- Patrols design and operate skill-based stalls at a public night market, teaching scouting skills to visitors while learning entrepreneurship, with proceeds going to a troop-chosen cause.

3. **Patrol Documentary Film Festival -- "Our Town Through Scout Eyes"** -- Patrols produce short documentary films about overlooked aspects of their local community over several weeks, culminating in a premiere night screening event with awards.

## 4. Evolution Through Rounds

### "Lost Expedition" (Idea 1) — WINNER

Started as an ambitious 6-8 week campaign with complex token economies and branching consequences, then was progressively streamlined across three rounds into a tight 4-5 week format with collective progress, hidden discovery moments, self-selecting challenge tiers, and clear rubrics.
