## Idea: "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Immersive Survival Campaign Game

### Description
Instead of running standalone activities each week, the troop leader designs a connected narrative campaign that spans 6-8 weekly meetings and culminates in a weekend camping trip. The premise: the scouts are members of a fictional expedition team whose supply ship has been lost. Each week they must master a new real survival or scouting skill to "progress" through the story, earning resources and clues that carry over to the next session. The narrative unfolds through sealed mission envelopes, short journal entries from a fictional expedition leader, and a large hand-drawn map that the troop fills in together over the weeks.

Each weekly session teaches a genuine skill framed as a story challenge. For example, Week 1 might involve orienteering and compass work ("You have washed ashore -- use these bearings to locate the supply cache"), Week 3 could be a fire-building competition ("Night is falling and temperatures are dropping -- only the patrols that get a fire lit in time earn warmth tokens"), and Week 5 might be a cooking challenge using limited, odd ingredients ("This is all the foraging party found -- make it work"). Patrols track their progress on a shared scoreboard using a resource system: food tokens, shelter tokens, morale tokens. Decisions the patrol makes each week -- do you spend time building better shelter or press on exploring? -- have real consequences in the following sessions. This gives the troop a reason to show up each week and creates genuine anticipation.

The campaign culminates in a weekend campout where all the skills come together. Patrols must navigate to a campsite using map skills they have practiced, build real shelters using lashing and tarp techniques from earlier sessions, cook a meal with constrained supplies, and complete a final nighttime orienteering challenge to "find rescue." The resources they banked during the weekly meetings translate into small real advantages at the campout -- extra tarp, a better cooking pot, first choice of campsite. This gives weight to the weeks of preparation without making the final event unfair.

The format works brilliantly for the 10-14 age range because it taps into the same psychology that makes video games and tabletop RPGs compelling for this age group: progression, narrative stakes, and meaningful choices. Scouts who might find a standalone knot-tying lesson dull become deeply engaged when that knot is the difference between their patrol's shelter holding up overnight or collapsing. The co-ed mixed-age format is also well served because the patrol system lets older scouts naturally mentor younger ones, and the variety of challenges (physical, intellectual, creative, social) means every scout gets a chance to be the hero of at least one session.

### Advantages
- Transforms routine skill-building meetings into an exciting ongoing story that scouts genuinely look forward to each week
- Teaches real, badge-relevant scouting skills (orienteering, fire-building, first aid, cooking, lashing, knot-tying) without them feeling like classroom lessons
- The carry-over resource system and consequential choices create genuine engagement and attendance motivation across multiple weeks
- Naturally accommodates mixed ages and genders because patrols need diverse strengths (strategy, physical ability, creativity, attention to detail)
- Culminates in a memorable campout where scouts apply everything they have learned, creating a powerful sense of achievement
- Low cost to run -- the leader mostly needs printed mission envelopes, a large map poster, and token cards, plus normal camping gear for the finale
- Highly customisable -- the leader can swap in whatever skills they want to teach and adjust difficulty based on the troop's experience level
- Encourages patrol identity and teamwork over individual competition, building lasting friendships

### Why This Idea Has Merit
Research from real scouts consistently shows that their most treasured memories come from multi-day adventures where skills come together in authentic contexts -- Philmont treks, canoe expeditions, and wilderness survival nights rank among the top experiences in crowdsourced lists. However, most troops cannot do high adventure every week. This idea captures that same sense of journey and stakes within the structure of regular weekly meetings. It draws on the proven appeal of gamification and narrative-driven experiences for the 10-14 age bracket, an age group deeply familiar with progression systems from gaming. The teamwork research also supports this approach: activities like escape rooms, cooking challenges, and building competitions are already recognised as top-tier scout team-building exercises, and this format strings them together with narrative glue that makes each one matter more. The campaign structure also solves a common troop leader headache -- planning feels less scattered when there is a throughline, and scouts who miss a week can be caught up quickly because the story provides context.
