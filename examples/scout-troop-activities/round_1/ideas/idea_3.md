## Idea: Patrol Documentary Film Festival -- "Our Town Through Scout Eyes"

### Description
Each patrol is tasked with producing a short documentary film (5-10 minutes) about a hidden or overlooked aspect of their local community. Topics could include a forgotten piece of local history, a wildlife habitat tucked behind a shopping centre, an elderly neighbour with an extraordinary life story, or a local trade or craft that is disappearing. The patrols have three to four weeks to research, film, edit, and present their documentaries, culminating in a "premiere night" screening event where families, friends, and community members are invited to watch all the films and vote on awards.

The activity unfolds across several weekly meetings with distinct phases. In week one, patrols brainstorm topics, learn basic storyboarding and interview techniques, and plan their shoots. The troop leader can bring in a local journalist, film student, or media teacher for a short workshop on framing shots, recording good audio with a smartphone, and asking open-ended interview questions. In weeks two and three, patrols go out into the community to film. This might involve visiting a local historical site, interviewing a veteran or shop owner, filming wildlife at dawn, or documenting a community garden. Scouts learn to work as a crew with distinct roles: director, camera operator, sound recorder, interviewer, and researcher. In week four, patrols edit their footage together using free tools like iMovie, CapCut, or DaVinci Resolve, add narration and titles, and prepare a brief live introduction for their film.

The premiere night is the centrepiece. Set up a meeting hall or outdoor space with a projector and screen, popcorn, and handmade "red carpet" decorations. Each patrol introduces their film, it screens, and then takes a few questions from the audience. Awards are given in multiple categories -- Best Story, Best Cinematography, Most Surprising Discovery, Best Interview, Audience Choice -- so every patrol has a realistic chance of winning something. The finished films can also be shared with the local library, historical society, or community social media pages, giving the scouts a genuine sense that their work has lasting value.

This activity is designed to appeal across the full 10-14 age range. Younger scouts can handle research, on-screen presenting, and creative tasks like making props or title cards, while older scouts take on editing, directing, and the technical side of filming. The mixed-age collaboration within patrols mirrors real film production crews where different skill levels contribute to a shared product.

### Advantages
- Teaches digital literacy and media production skills that scouts rarely encounter in traditional programming but that are highly relevant to their daily lives
- Gets scouts out into their community in an investigative, purposeful way -- they are not just passing through but actively engaging with people and places
- Naturally accommodates the full 10-14 age range by offering roles at different skill levels within each patrol
- Creates a tangible, shareable product that scouts can be proud of long after the activity ends
- The premiere night is a memorable social event that strengthens troop culture and involves families and the wider community
- Develops communication, planning, teamwork, and time-management skills across multiple weeks without requiring a wilderness setting or specialised equipment
- Can be done in any season and in urban, suburban, or rural settings with nothing more than smartphones scouts already have access to

### Why This Idea Has Merit
Research from real scout troops consistently highlights that the most memorable activities combine genuine skill-building with a sense of adventure and a meaningful end product. The crowdsourced list of 99 favourite BSA activities includes urban hiking historical tours and creative challenges as some of the most cherished experiences, showing that scouts do not need to be in the backcountry to feel engaged. The resource on game ideas for older scouts specifically recommends activities like filming public service announcements and producing commercials, noting that video production sparks creativity and presentation skills -- but a full documentary project takes this much further by adding research, community engagement, and narrative structure. Meanwhile, the teamwork activities research emphasises that the strongest team-building comes from projects where every member has a distinct, necessary role -- exactly what a film crew provides. By connecting scouts to overlooked stories in their own community, this activity also builds the civic awareness and service orientation that scouting values, while producing something the community itself can benefit from and appreciate.
