## Evaluation: "Lost Expedition" -- A Multi-Week Immersive Survival Campaign Game

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Fun | 6 | The concept is appealing in theory but the actual fun depends heavily on execution quality the idea cannot guarantee. |
| Simplicity | 3 | A 6-8 week campaign with resource tokens, branching consequences, narrative envelopes, and a shared map is deeply complex to explain and run. |
| Challenge | 7 | Genuinely covers a wide range of real scouting skills in contexts that give them purpose. |
| Social Factor | 6 | Patrol-based teamwork is solid but the competitive resource system risks fragmenting rather than unifying the troop. |
| **Aggregate** | **5.5** | |

### Critical Analysis

#### Fun (Score: 6)
The idea claims it taps into the psychology of video games and tabletop RPGs, but this comparison is misleading. Video games deliver instant feedback loops, polished aesthetics, and tight pacing -- none of which a volunteer troop leader printing mission envelopes can replicate. The fun of this activity is almost entirely contingent on the leader being a skilled storyteller and game designer, which is an enormous unstated dependency. A dry or disorganised leader will produce something that feels like a normal meeting with awkward narrative wrapping paper.

The 6-8 week commitment is a serious fun risk. If the first session does not land well, scouts are stuck in a campaign that has lost momentum. Video games let you quit; a troop meeting does not. The sealed envelopes and fictional journal entries sound charming on paper, but for 10-14 year olds who are used to high-production-value entertainment, handwritten notes from a fictional expedition leader could easily feel corny rather than exciting. The idea provides no evidence that this narrative framing actually sustains engagement over two months -- it simply asserts it will.

The resource token system could also become tedious rather than exciting. Tracking food tokens, shelter tokens, and morale tokens across weeks introduces bookkeeping that 10-year-olds are unlikely to find thrilling. The "meaningful choices" (spend time on shelter or explore?) sound compelling in a game design document but may feel arbitrary in practice when scouts do not fully understand the downstream consequences.

#### Simplicity (Score: 3)
This is the idea's most glaring weakness. The proposal asks a leader to explain to a room of 10-14 year olds: a multi-week narrative arc, a resource token economy with multiple token types, a consequence system where decisions carry over between sessions, patrol-based scoring on a shared scoreboard, sealed mission envelopes with a fiction layer, and a culminating campout where banked resources convert to real advantages. That is not simple. That is a tabletop RPG campaign with a rulebook.

The idea claims it is "highly customisable" and "low cost to run," but customisability is the opposite of simplicity -- it means the leader must make dozens of design decisions before the first session. What are the resource conversion rates? How do missed weeks work? What happens if a patrol loses all their tokens early? None of this is addressed, because the idea is a high-level concept, not a ready-to-run activity.

For the scouts themselves, the cognitive overhead of tracking an ongoing narrative, remembering what resources they have, understanding the consequences of choices, and connecting this week's activity to last week's story is substantial. A 10-year-old who missed Week 3 will be lost by Week 5 regardless of how quickly the story "provides context." The claim that missed scouts can be caught up easily is entirely unsupported.

#### Challenge (Score: 7)
This is the idea's strongest dimension. It genuinely covers orienteering, fire-building, cooking under constraints, shelter construction, lashing, and knot-tying -- all real scouting skills. Framing them as story challenges does plausibly add motivation to learn them properly. The weekend campout as a skills integration exercise is a sound pedagogical approach.

However, the idea is vague about how skill difficulty scales across the 10-14 age range. A compass exercise that challenges a 10-year-old will bore a 14-year-old, and vice versa. The claim that "patrols need diverse strengths" hand-waves this problem without solving it. The idea also does not address what happens when a patrol consistently fails challenges -- do they fall so far behind in resources that the final campout becomes demoralising rather than challenging? The competitive resource system could punish weaker or younger patrols cumulatively across eight weeks, turning meaningful challenge into discouragement.

The skill coverage is also front-loaded with outdoor and physical skills. The idea mentions "intellectual" and "creative" challenges in passing but only provides concrete examples of traditional bushcraft activities. For scouts whose strengths are not physical or outdoors-oriented, the challenge dimension may be narrower than claimed.

#### Social Factor (Score: 6)
The patrol system is a reasonable foundation for teamwork, and the idea correctly identifies that mixed-age patrols can enable mentorship. However, the competitive resource economy works against troop-wide social cohesion. Patrols competing for tokens, advantages, and scoreboard position creates in-group bonding at the expense of cross-patrol relationships. The final campout rewards -- extra tarp, better cooking pot, first choice of campsite -- explicitly give winning patrols material advantages over losing ones, which could breed resentment rather than camaraderie.

The idea claims the format "encourages patrol identity and teamwork over individual competition," but patrol identity IS group competition. A troop of 20 scouts split into four competing patrols for eight weeks will develop strong patrol loyalty but potentially weaker troop unity. The idea does not include any mechanism for cross-patrol collaboration or troop-wide social bonding.

The claim that "every scout gets a chance to be the hero of at least one session" is asserted without any structural guarantee. In practice, the most confident and vocal scouts tend to dominate group activities, and the idea provides no mechanism to ensure quieter or younger scouts get meaningful leadership moments. The narrative framing could even make this worse -- in a story-driven context, natural leaders tend to assume protagonist roles while others become passive participants.
