## Evaluation: Patrol Documentary Film Festival -- "Our Town Through Scout Eyes"

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Fun | 4 | The activity is more akin to a school project than a thrilling scout outing, with the bulk of time spent on planning, filming logistics, and editing software. |
| Simplicity | 3 | Explaining storyboarding, interview technique, camera framing, audio recording, and video editing to a room of 10-14 year olds is a multi-session briefing, not a simple explanation. |
| Challenge | 7 | Scouts genuinely learn transferable skills in media production, interviewing, research, and project management across multiple weeks. |
| Social Factor | 6 | Patrol-based crew roles create interdependence, but much of the actual work (editing, filming on location) can easily fragment into one or two competent scouts doing everything while others watch. |
| **Aggregate** | **5.0** | |

### Critical Analysis

#### Fun (Score: 4)
The idea leans heavily on the premiere night as the exciting payoff, but the three to four weeks leading up to it are dominated by activities that closely resemble homework: researching local history, planning storyboards, learning interview techniques, and wrestling with editing software. For 10-14 year olds who come to scouts expecting physical activity, adventure, and hands-on excitement, sitting in front of a laptop trimming clips in DaVinci Resolve is unlikely to generate laughter or genuine thrill. The description itself inadvertently reveals this problem -- filming wildlife "at dawn" or interviewing an elderly neighbour are experiences that may be enriching but are not what most adolescents would describe as fun. The premiere night with popcorn and a red carpet is a single evening after weeks of effort, and even that event is essentially sitting and watching videos, which is passive entertainment rather than active engagement. The proposal does not address the very real risk that large portions of the troop will be bored during the production phases, especially younger scouts who are assigned "creative tasks like making props or title cards" -- essentially busywork. Comparing this to activities like wide games, campouts, or obstacle courses, the excitement gap is stark.

#### Simplicity (Score: 3)
This is one of the most complex activity proposals imaginable for a scout troop. It requires explaining and teaching: storyboarding, interview technique, camera framing, audio recording, video editing software, narrative structure, crew roles, and project scheduling -- all before the actual activity can begin. The idea explicitly calls for bringing in an external expert (journalist, film student, or media teacher) in week one, which is itself an admission that the leaders cannot simply explain this to the room. The multi-week phased structure with distinct tasks each week adds scheduling complexity. Leaders need to coordinate community visits, manage permissions for filming and interviewing strangers, ensure access to editing software and devices, and orchestrate the premiere event with a projector, screen, and invited audience. The idea claims it can be done "with nothing more than smartphones" but then references iMovie, CapCut, and DaVinci Resolve, which require laptops or tablets and a baseline level of technical literacy that not all scouts or leaders possess. Explaining this activity to a room of fidgeting 10-14 year olds in a way they all understand and can act on is not realistic in a single briefing -- it requires repeated instruction across multiple sessions.

#### Challenge (Score: 7)
This is the strongest dimension for the idea. Scouts would genuinely develop skills in video production, interviewing, research, storytelling, and project management -- competencies that are rarely addressed in traditional scouting and are relevant to modern life. The multi-week timeline creates sustained challenge rather than a one-off task. The crew role structure means different scouts face different challenges (technical, interpersonal, creative). However, the score is held back by the fact that the challenge is unevenly distributed. The idea acknowledges that younger scouts get simpler tasks like "making props or title cards," which are not meaningfully challenging. The editing and directing roles -- where the real skill development happens -- are reserved for older scouts, meaning a 10-year-old may go through the entire project without being genuinely stretched. Additionally, the challenge is heavily cognitive and creative but includes almost no physical or outdoor skill development, which is a notable gap for a scouting context where the whole-person development model typically includes physical challenge.

#### Social Factor (Score: 6)
The patrol crew structure is sound in theory -- distinct roles creating interdependence mirrors effective team-building design. However, the idea underestimates a well-documented problem in group projects with adolescents: uneven participation. In practice, the one or two scouts who are already comfortable with technology will dominate the editing and directing, while less confident members drift into passive roles. The idea does not describe any mechanism to prevent this. Community interviews and location filming do get scouts interacting with people outside their troop, which is valuable, but much of the intra-patrol collaboration happens in small subgroups rather than the full troop together, which limits the broader troop bonding effect. The premiere night is a shared social event, but it is competitive (patrols voting on awards), which can create inter-patrol rivalry rather than troop-wide trust and friendship. The three to four week timeline also means that scouts who miss a meeting can become disconnected from their patrol's project, fracturing rather than strengthening group cohesion. The proposal claims this builds collaboration and teamwork but provides no concrete structure (beyond assigning role titles) to ensure that collaboration actually happens in a meaningful, trust-building way.
