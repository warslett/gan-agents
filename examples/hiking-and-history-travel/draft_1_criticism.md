# Criticism of Draft 1

## Idea 1: The Lycian Way, Turkey

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) |
|---|---|
| Fun — How much day-to-day enjoyment and excitement will the trip provide? | 8 |
| Cost — How much experience and quality do you get relative to what you spend? | 9 |
| Challenge — How much will the trip push you out of your comfort zone overall (language, culture, navigation)? | 7 |
| Safety — How low-risk is the destination across all factors: crime, health, terrain, and infrastructure? | 6 |
| Climate — How suitable is the climate for hiking? Temperatures hotter than 25 degrees Celsius will be uncomfortable. | 7 |
| **Aggregate** | **7.4** |

### Critical Analysis
The Lycian Way is a genuinely strong proposal that integrates hiking and history in a way few trails can match — the ruins are not detours but waypoints on the trail itself. The cost-to-quality ratio is outstanding, and the proposal wisely identifies spring and autumn as the ideal windows.

However, several weaknesses need scrutiny. First, the climate score is tempered by risk: even in spring, sections of the Mediterranean coast can spike above 25 degrees Celsius in late May, and the trail has long exposed stretches with minimal shade on the coastal segments. The proposal says "15-23 degrees" but that represents the average; afternoon peaks on south-facing cliffs can be materially hotter. Second, trail waymarking on the Lycian Way is inconsistent — it has improved in recent years but there remain sections where paint markers are faded or missing, and navigation errors can add hours to a day. The proposal does not mention this at all. Third, regarding safety, Turkey is broadly safe for tourists, but the trail passes through remote areas with limited mobile phone coverage and no mountain rescue infrastructure comparable to Western Europe. Medical facilities in the small villages along the route are basic. The political situation in Turkey, while stable in the southwest, adds a layer of unpredictability that does not apply to Western European or Anglophone destinations. Finally, the cultural and language challenge is moderate — English is widely spoken in tourist areas along the coast, but in the mountain villages away from the main tourist corridor, communication can be difficult. The proposal slightly oversells the ease of the experience.

### Verdict: Strong

## Idea 2: The Scottish Highlands — West Highland Way and Beyond

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) |
|---|---|
| Fun — How much day-to-day enjoyment and excitement will the trip provide? | 6 |
| Cost — How much experience and quality do you get relative to what you spend? | 5 |
| Challenge — How much will the trip push you out of your comfort zone overall (language, culture, navigation)? | 3 |
| Safety — How low-risk is the destination across all factors: crime, health, terrain, and infrastructure? | 9 |
| Climate — How suitable is the climate for hiking? Temperatures hotter than 25 degrees Celsius will be uncomfortable. | 7 |
| **Aggregate** | **6.0** |

### Critical Analysis
Scotland is a safe, accessible, and well-infrastructured destination. The climate is genuinely excellent for hiking in terms of temperature — you will almost never be too hot. The West Highland Way is a well-maintained trail with good facilities.

The problems, however, are significant. First, the fun score is dragged down by the Scottish weather reality that the proposal glosses over with a breezy "you should always be prepared for rain." Scottish Highlands summer weather is not occasionally rainy — it is frequently rainy. Statistical averages show 15-20 rain days per month in the western Highlands during summer. Midges (biting insects) are endemic from June to September and can make outdoor activity genuinely miserable in still, damp conditions, particularly around dawn and dusk. The proposal does not mention midges at all, which is a glaring omission. Second, the cost score is poor. Scotland is expensive — accommodation along the West Highland Way is in high demand during summer and prices are correspondingly inflated. A three-week trip as proposed would be substantially more expensive than either Turkey or Peru for a comparable standard of accommodation and food. Wild camping is legal but requires gear investment and tolerance for wet conditions. Third, the challenge score is very low. Scotland is culturally, linguistically, and logistically almost identical to the rest of the UK. There is no language barrier, no navigational complexity beyond following a well-marked trail, and no cultural adjustment. For a traveller seeking to be pushed out of their comfort zone, Scotland offers almost nothing. Fourth, while the history is genuinely rich, it is largely interpreted through museums and visitor centres rather than immersive on-trail experiences. The West Highland Way itself passes through dramatic landscape but relatively few historical sites directly — you need to drive or take transport to reach Culloden, Stirling, and other key sites, fragmenting the hiking-and-history integration.

### Verdict: Weak

## Idea 3: Peru — The Inca Trail and Sacred Valley

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) |
|---|---|
| Fun — How much day-to-day enjoyment and excitement will the trip provide? | 9 |
| Cost — How much experience and quality do you get relative to what you spend? | 7 |
| Challenge — How much will the trip push you out of your comfort zone overall (language, culture, navigation)? | 9 |
| Safety — How low-risk is the destination across all factors: crime, health, terrain, and infrastructure? | 5 |
| Climate — How suitable is the climate for hiking? Temperatures hotter than 25 degrees Celsius will be uncomfortable. | 8 |
| **Aggregate** | **7.6** |

### Critical Analysis
Peru is the most exciting and ambitious proposal. The fusion of hiking and history is extraordinary — the Inca Trail is literally an ancient road, and arriving at Machu Picchu on foot after four days of trekking is a bucket-list experience with good reason. The challenge score is high and justified: Spanish is the primary language (Quechua in rural areas), the culture is profoundly different, altitude sickness is a real navigational and physical challenge, and logistics in Peru require genuine adaptability. The climate at altitude is excellent for hiking.

However, the weaknesses are real. Safety is the primary concern. Altitude sickness affects a significant proportion of visitors to Cusco (3,400 metres) and the high passes (above 4,200 metres) — it can range from headaches and nausea to life-threatening pulmonary or cerebral oedema. The proposal mentions "genuine physical challenge" but significantly understates the medical risk. Petty crime in Cusco is common — pickpocketing, bag-snatching, and taxi scams are well-documented. The Inca Trail itself requires a licensed guide and permits that must be booked months in advance; the proposal does not mention this logistical constraint. Road safety in Peru is poor by Western standards, particularly on mountain roads. Regarding cost, while Peru is cheap on the ground, the Inca Trail permit and mandatory guided trek costs approximately $600-800 USD per person, and international flights to Lima from Europe or North America are expensive. The "very affordable" claim needs qualification. Finally, the proposal mentions the Ausangate Circuit as an option but does not adequately flag that this is a serious high-altitude trek (reaching 5,200 metres) requiring prior acclimatisation and good fitness — it is not a casual extension.

### Verdict: Strong

---

## Eviction Recommendation

**Evict: Idea 2 — The Scottish Highlands — West Highland Way and Beyond**

**Reason:** Scotland scores lowest across the aggregate (6.0) and is critically weak on Challenge (3/10) and Cost (5/10). The proposal fails to mention midges, understates the frequency of rain, and overstates the integration of hiking and history — key historical sites require separate transport rather than being on the trail. While it excels on Safety (9/10), this single strength cannot compensate for mediocrity or weakness across the other four dimensions. Both Turkey and Peru offer materially better value, more adventure, and tighter integration of hiking with historical experience.
