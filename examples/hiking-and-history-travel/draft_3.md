# Draft 3 — Hiking and History Travel Destination

## Idea 1: Peru — The Inca Trail and Sacred Valley

### Description
Embark on a three-week trip through Peru centred on the Inca Trail to Machu Picchu, expanded with hiking and historical exploration across the Sacred Valley and beyond. The classic four-day Inca Trail (43 kilometres) crosses three high-altitude passes above 4,000 metres, passing through cloud forest and dozens of Inca ruins before the iconic sunrise arrival at Machu Picchu. Before or after the trail, explore Cusco — a UNESCO World Heritage city built on Inca foundations — the massive fortress of Sacsayhuaman, the salt terraces of Maras, and the agricultural laboratory of Moray.

### Suggested Itinerary (18-21 days)

**Days 1-2: Lima.** Arrive and recover from the flight. Explore the historic centre (Plaza Mayor, catacombs of San Francisco monastery), the world-class Larco Museum (pre-Columbian art), and eat at Lima's celebrated restaurants. Altitude: sea level — no acclimatisation pressure.

**Days 3-5: Sacred Valley (Ollantaytambo and Pisac).** Travel overland from Lima to the Sacred Valley (bus to Cusco then collectivo down, or direct tourist bus). Base yourself in Ollantaytambo (2,792 metres) for initial acclimatisation. Explore the Ollantaytambo fortress ruins, hike to the Pinkuylluna granaries opposite, and visit the Pisac archaeological site and its famous Sunday market. Day hike options: the trail from Ollantaytambo to the Inca quarries above the town, or the Huchuy Qosqo trek (moderate, one day, reaching 3,600 metres).

**Days 6-8: Cusco.** Ascend to Cusco (3,400 metres) after three days of acclimatisation. Explore the city: Qorikancha (the Inca sun temple beneath the Spanish church of Santo Domingo), Sacsayhuaman's massive stone walls, the San Pedro Market, and the bohemian quarter of San Blas. Visit the Maras salt terraces and the concentric rings of Moray on a day trip. Allow time for your body to adjust — keep activity moderate.

**Days 9-12: The Inca Trail.** Begin the four-day guided trek from Kilometre 82. Day 1 follows the Urubamba River to Wayllabamba. Day 2 is the hardest — ascending to Dead Woman's Pass (4,215 metres) through high-altitude grassland. Day 3 traverses the ridge with views of snow-capped Salkantay and descends through cloud forest past the ruins of Phuyupatamarca and Winay Wayna. Day 4: the pre-dawn walk to the Sun Gate and the first view of Machu Picchu below.

**Days 13-14: Machu Picchu and Aguas Calientes.** Full day exploring Machu Picchu. Hike Huayna Picchu or Machu Picchu Mountain for aerial views (separate permit required, book in advance). Return to Cusco by train and bus.

**Days 15-17: Lares Trek (optional alternative/supplement).** A 3-day trek through the Lares Valley, passing through remote Quechua-speaking communities known for traditional weaving. This trek is less touristed than the Inca Trail and offers genuine cultural immersion. Maximum altitude approximately 4,400 metres — manageable if you are now fully acclimatised.

**Days 18-21: Cusco, Lake Titicaca, or return.** Options include: further exploration of Cusco, a trip to Lake Titicaca and the Uros floating islands (combining history with high-altitude landscape), or travel to the Colca Canyon for condor viewing and canyon hiking. Alternatively, begin the journey home via Lima.

### Permits and Logistics
The Inca Trail is strictly regulated. Only 500 people per day are permitted on the trail (including guides and porters), and permits must be booked 4-6 months in advance through a licensed tour operator. The mandatory guided four-day trek costs approximately $600-800 USD per person including permits, guide, porters, meals, and camping equipment. This is a significant fixed cost that should be budgeted for upfront. Book as early as possible — June-August permits sell out fastest. The Huayna Picchu climb permit ($75 USD) also sells out months ahead.

### Altitude Sickness — A Serious Consideration
Cusco sits at 3,400 metres. The Inca Trail crosses Dead Woman's Pass at 4,215 metres. Altitude sickness (acute mountain sickness, or AMS) affects an estimated 25-50% of visitors to Cusco to some degree. Symptoms range from headaches, nausea, and fatigue to, in rare but serious cases, pulmonary or cerebral oedema which can be life-threatening.

**Mitigation strategy:** The itinerary above is designed around acclimatisation — arriving at sea level in Lima, spending three days in the Sacred Valley at 2,800 metres, then ascending to Cusco at 3,400 metres. This graduated approach significantly reduces risk. Consider carrying acetazolamide (Diamox) after consulting a travel doctor before departure. Coca tea is widely available locally and provides mild symptomatic relief. Know the symptoms and have a plan to descend immediately if they worsen. Do NOT dismiss altitude as a minor inconvenience — it is the single biggest health risk of this trip.

### Safety
Petty crime (pickpocketing, bag-snatching) is common in Cusco, particularly around the Plaza de Armas and San Pedro Market. Use a money belt, avoid displaying expensive equipment, and take only registered taxis (or use apps like inDrive). Road safety on mountain roads is poor by Western standards — if travelling by bus, choose reputable companies (Cruz del Sur, Oltursa) over budget operators. On the Inca Trail itself, safety is well-managed by the licensed guide system. Travel insurance covering high-altitude trekking and emergency evacuation is essential.

### Budget Estimate (per person, approximate)
- International flights (from Europe): $800-1,200 USD
- Inca Trail guided trek (4 days, all-inclusive): $600-800 USD
- Accommodation (16-17 nights at $15-30 USD/night): $250-500 USD
- Food and drink (outside trek, $10-20 USD/day): $170-340 USD
- Internal transport (buses, trains, collectivos): $150-250 USD
- Entrance fees (Machu Picchu, ruins, museums): $100-150 USD
- **Estimated total: $2,070-3,240 USD for 3 weeks**

### Climate
The dry season (May-September) offers the best conditions. At altitude, daytime temperatures are typically 15-20 degrees Celsius — ideal for hiking and never uncomfortably hot. Nights can drop below freezing above 4,000 metres, so pack thermal base layers, a down jacket, and a four-season sleeping bag (or confirm your tour operator provides one rated for cold). June and July offer the driest and clearest weather but are also the busiest months.

### Advantages
- Machu Picchu is one of the most iconic historical sites on Earth, and arriving on foot via the Inca Trail is profoundly memorable
- The Inca civilisation's engineering and architectural achievements are staggering and well-preserved at altitude
- Hiking at high altitude provides a genuine physical and mental challenge and a deep sense of accomplishment
- Cusco and the Sacred Valley offer deep cultural immersion — living Quechua traditions, vibrant markets, and world-class Peruvian cuisine
- Once on the ground, Peru is affordable — meals cost $3-8 USD, guesthouse accommodation $15-30 USD per night
- The dry season climate is near-perfect for hiking: cool, clear, and dry, with no risk of temperatures exceeding 25 degrees Celsius at the altitudes you will be walking
- The cultural and linguistic challenge (Spanish, Quechua, unfamiliar logistics) makes this a genuinely transformative travel experience
- The itinerary offers extraordinary variety: cosmopolitan Lima, colonial Cusco, ancient ruins, mountain passes, cloud forests, indigenous villages, and world-class cuisine

### Why This Idea Has Merit
Peru represents perhaps the ultimate fusion of hiking and history — the trails themselves were built by the Inca Empire, and the destination is one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. The physical challenge of high-altitude trekking adds a dimension that flatter historical walks cannot match. The cultural experience extends beyond ruins into living indigenous traditions, and the sheer drama of the Andean landscape — snow-capped peaks, cloud forests, and terraced mountainsides — creates an unforgettable backdrop. With proper acclimatisation planning (built into the itinerary), permit booking, safety awareness, and realistic budgeting, the risks are manageable and the rewards are extraordinary. This is a trip that changes how you see the world.
