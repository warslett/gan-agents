## Idea: Transylvania Circuit -- Carpathian Peaks, Fortified Churches, and Medieval Citadels

### Description
Spend one week tracing a loop through Transylvania in Romania, starting and ending in Brasov, combining daily hiking with an extraordinary density of medieval, Saxon, and Ottoman history. Romania is one of Europe's most underrated destinations for this combination: the Carpathian Mountains offer alpine-grade trails with cool summer temperatures (15-22 degrees Celsius at altitude), while the lowland towns of Transylvania preserve some of Europe's best-intact medieval architecture -- fortified churches, citadels, and old towns layered with centuries of history. Unlike more polished Western European destinations, Transylvania retains a raw quality -- shepherds still move flocks along valley roads, villages like Viscri have changed little in centuries, and you are as likely to share a trail with a local forester as with another tourist.

**Days 1-3: Brasov and the Carpathian High Country.** Day one explores Brasov's remarkable old town -- the Black Church (the largest Gothic church between Vienna and Istanbul), Council Square, and the Tampa Mountain trail (1.5 hours, 400m elevation gain) for panoramic views over terracotta rooftops backed by forested mountains. Day two is a full day in Piatra Craiului National Park, beginning with the Zarnesti Gorge and ascending the dramatic limestone ridge. This is a serious mountain day: five to seven hours of hiking, graded as "difficult" by Romanian trail standards, involving exposed sections above the treeline with steep drop-offs. Hikers should be comfortable with sustained ascent (approximately 1,000m elevation gain), carry layered clothing for rapidly changing alpine weather, and bring sturdy hiking boots with ankle support. The reward is one of the most spectacular ridge walks in Eastern Europe, with potential wildlife sightings in one of the continent's last great wilderness corridors. Day three offers a choice of two hikes: either the Postavaru Peak trail from Brasov (4-5 hours, moderate) for sweeping views across the Carpathian range, or the Bucegi Plateau via cable car from Busteni with hiking among dramatic wind-carved rock formations (the Sphinx, Babele) at 2,000m -- both options deliver a full day on the trail.

**Days 4-5: The Saxon Heartland on Foot.** Rather than rushing through by car, these days integrate hiking with historical exploration. Day four travels to Viscri, a tiny village with a UNESCO-listed fortified church famously championed by King Charles III, and includes a half-day hike through the surrounding pastoral hills and oak forests -- rolling terrain with wildflower meadows that connect scattered Saxon villages (3-4 hours, easy-to-moderate). This is where Transylvania's "undiscovered" quality is most tangible: no tour buses, no gift shops, just centuries-old farmhouses and the sound of church bells. The day ends in Sighisoara -- the birthplace of Vlad Tepes (the historical Dracula) and one of the best-preserved inhabited medieval citadels in all of Europe, with its 14th-century clock tower and cobbled streets best explored on foot in the evening light. Day five continues to Sibiu, a former European Capital of Culture with a stunning Germanic old town and the Brukenthal Museum (one of the oldest museums in Europe, founded 1790), followed by an afternoon hike along the Sub Cetate trail or in the forested hills above the city (2-3 hours), offering a perspective on Sibiu most visitors never see.

**Days 6-7: Alpine Finale.** Day six drives a section of the Transfagarasan Highway (open typically June through October) -- one of the world's most dramatic mountain roads -- to Balea Lake at 2,034 metres, with a hike up to the Balea ridgeline or onward toward the Negoiu Peak approach trail (4-6 hours, moderate-to-difficult). If the Transfagarasan is closed, the strong alternative is the Cindrel Mountains above Sibiu, a quieter range with excellent ridge hiking and panoramic views (Cindrelul Mare summit, 2,244m, 5-6 hours). Both options are genuine full-day mountain hikes, not a downgrade. Day seven returns to Brasov with a morning hike at Rasnov Fortress -- a 13th-century hilltop citadel built by Teutonic Knights -- combining the historical visit with the trail network in the surrounding forests (2 hours), before a final afternoon in Brasov.

**Safety and Practical Considerations.** This itinerary passes through areas with significant brown bear populations, particularly around Piatra Craiului and the broader Brasov region. Bear encounters on trails, while uncommon, are a real possibility. Hikers should carry bear spray (available in Brasov outdoor shops), make noise on the trail, hike in groups where possible, and know basic bear encounter protocol (do not run, back away slowly, make yourself appear large). On ridge trails, mobile phone signal can be patchy; download offline maps (maps.me or the Salvamont app, Romania's mountain rescue service) before setting out. For the more challenging days (Piatra Craiului ridge, Balea/Cindrel), hikers should be in good physical condition and carry sufficient water, sun protection, and a waterproof layer -- weather above 1,500m can change rapidly. A rental car is strongly recommended for this itinerary, as public transport between smaller stops is impractical on this timescale. Budget approximately 200-300 euros for a week's car rental including fuel and insurance. Romanian mountain roads require attentive driving, particularly on the Transfagarasan with its hairpin turns at altitude; drive during daylight hours and watch for livestock on rural roads. The nearest well-equipped hospitals are in Brasov and Sibiu, both of which are visited during the trip. Romania's mountain rescue service (Salvamont) can be reached at 0-SALVAMONT (0725-826668) and maintains stations near major trailheads.

What makes this trip exceptional is the layering of history. Transylvania is not about one era -- it is a palimpsest of Dacian, Roman, Saxon, Hungarian, Ottoman, Habsburg, and Romanian influences compressed into a compact region. You walk through a 13th-century church built by Saxon colonists, drive past a fortress raised against Ottoman raids, and hike mountains where Dacian warriors resisted the Roman Empire two thousand years ago. All of this comes at a genuinely low cost: budget 700 to 1,100 euros per person for the full week including car rental, accommodation in guesthouses and pensions, meals, fuel, and entry fees. Even at the upper end, this is roughly half the price of comparable trips in Austria or Norway and a third the price of Switzerland, while offering hiking and historical richness that rivals any of them.

### Advantages
- Genuinely affordable at 700-1,100 euros per person for the full week including car rental, still roughly half the cost of Western European alternatives
- Cool mountain hiking temperatures of 15-22 degrees Celsius in summer, well within the comfort zone for heat-averse travellers
- Hiking every single day of the trip, ranging from two-hour cultural walks to serious five-to-seven-hour alpine ridge days, ensuring consistent trail time
- Extraordinary historical depth spanning Dacian, Roman, Saxon, Ottoman, and Habsburg eras in a single compact region
- Multiple UNESCO World Heritage Sites including fortified churches, medieval citadels, and Sighisoara's old town
- Genuine off-the-beaten-path feel with far fewer tourists than Scotland, Norway, or the Alps, substantiated by village experiences like Viscri where tourism infrastructure barely exists
- Unique wildlife corridor -- one of the last places in Europe with viable populations of brown bears, wolves, and lynx in unfenced wilderness
- Strong alternative options built into the itinerary (Cindrel Mountains if Transfagarasan is closed, multiple day-three choices) so no day depends on a single weather-dependent plan
- Compact geography means minimal travel time between stops, maximising time spent hiking and exploring rather than driving
- Concrete safety planning including bear precautions, mountain rescue contacts, and gear guidance addresses the real risks of hiking in a wilder landscape
