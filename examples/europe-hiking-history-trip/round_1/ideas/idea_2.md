## Idea: Transylvania Circuit -- Carpathian Peaks, Fortified Churches, and Medieval Citadels

### Description
Spend one week tracing a loop through Transylvania in Romania, starting and ending in Brasov, combining serious mountain hiking in the Carpathians with an extraordinary density of medieval, Saxon, and Ottoman history. Romania is one of Europe's most underrated destinations for this combination: the Carpathian Mountains offer alpine-grade trails with cool summer temperatures (15-22 degrees Celsius at altitude), while the lowland towns of Transylvania preserve some of Europe's best-intact medieval architecture -- fortified churches, citadels, and old towns that feel frozen in time. Unlike more polished Western European destinations, Transylvania retains a raw, undiscovered quality that makes exploration genuinely exciting.

The itinerary would begin with two days based in Brasov, using the first day to explore the city's remarkable old town -- the Black Church (the largest Gothic church between Vienna and Istanbul), Council Square, and the Tampa Mountain hike for panoramic views over terracotta rooftops backed by forested mountains. Day two heads to Bran Castle, the fortress popularly linked to the Dracula legend, and Rasnov Fortress, a 13th-century hilltop citadel built by Teutonic Knights, with an afternoon hike in the Piatra Craiului foothills. Day three is a full mountain day in Piatra Craiului National Park, tackling the Zarnesti Gorge and the dramatic limestone ridge trails -- five to seven hours of challenging, rewarding hiking with potential brown bear sightings in one of Europe's last great wilderness areas.

The middle section of the week transitions northwest through the Saxon heartland. Day four travels via Viscri, a tiny village with a UNESCO-listed fortified church famously championed by King Charles III, to Sighisoara -- the birthplace of Vlad Tepes (the historical Dracula) and one of the best-preserved inhabited medieval citadels in all of Europe, complete with a 14th-century clock tower and cobbled streets. Day five continues to Sibiu, a former European Capital of Culture with a stunning Germanic old town, the Brukenthal Museum (one of the oldest museums in Europe, founded 1790), and the extraordinary ASTRA open-air ethnographic museum showcasing traditional Transylvanian village life across centuries. Day six offers a choice: either drive a section of the legendary Transfagarasan Highway (if open, typically June through October) -- one of the world's most dramatic mountain roads -- with hiking at Balea Lake at 2,034 metres, or explore the gentler Marginimea Sibiului region of traditional shepherd villages. Day seven returns to Brasov with a final stop at a fortified church or a morning hike.

What makes this trip exceptional is the layering of history. Transylvania is not about one era -- it is a palimpsest of Dacian, Roman, Saxon, Hungarian, Ottoman, Habsburg, and Romanian influences compressed into a compact region. You walk through a 13th-century church built by Saxon colonists, drive past a fortress raised against Ottoman raids, and hike mountains where Dacian warriors resisted the Roman Empire two thousand years ago. All of this comes at a fraction of the cost of Western European alternatives -- research indicates a budget of 500 to 800 euros per person for the full week, making it roughly half the price of comparable trips in Austria or Norway and a third the price of Switzerland.

### Advantages
- Remarkably affordable at 500-800 euros per person for the full week, roughly half the cost of Western European alternatives
- Cool mountain hiking temperatures of 15-22 degrees Celsius in summer, well within the comfort zone for heat-averse travellers
- Extraordinary historical depth spanning Dacian, Roman, Saxon, Ottoman, and Habsburg eras in a single compact region
- Multiple UNESCO World Heritage Sites including fortified churches, medieval citadels, and Sighisoara's old town
- Genuine off-the-beaten-path feel with far fewer tourists than Scotland, Norway, or the Alps, making for more authentic encounters
- Diverse hiking ranging from gentle town walks to serious alpine ridge trails in Piatra Craiului National Park
- Unique wildlife opportunities including brown bears, wolves, and lynx in one of Europe's last large wilderness corridors
- Compact geography means minimal travel time between stops, maximising time spent hiking and exploring rather than driving
