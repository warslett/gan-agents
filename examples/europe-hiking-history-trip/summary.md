# Summary — Europe Hiking and History Trip

## 1. Original Problem

> "Where should I go travelling this year for one week in Europe if I enjoy hiking and history but don't like very hot weather?"

[Research undertaken first](research/index.md)

**Scoring Dimensions:**
- **Fun** — How much day-to-day enjoyment and excitement does this trip provide while you're there?
- **Cost** — How much quality experience do you get relative to what you spend, regardless of absolute price?
- **Safety** — How low-risk is the entire trip, combining personal safety, health infrastructure, hiking hazards, and travel logistics?
- **Challenge** — How much does the trip push you out of your comfort zone and help you to grow as a person?

## 2. Final Idea

### Transylvania Circuit -- Carpathian Peaks, Fortified Churches, and Medieval Citadels

Spend one week tracing a loop through Transylvania in Romania, starting and ending in Brasov, combining daily hiking with an extraordinary density of medieval, Saxon, and Ottoman history. Romania is one of Europe's most underrated destinations for this combination: the Carpathian Mountains offer alpine-grade trails with cool summer temperatures (15-22 degrees Celsius at altitude), while the lowland towns of Transylvania preserve some of Europe's best-intact medieval architecture -- fortified churches, citadels, and old towns layered with centuries of history.

**Days 1-3: Brasov and the Carpathian High Country.** Day one explores Brasov's remarkable old town -- the Black Church (the largest Gothic church between Vienna and Istanbul), Council Square, and the Tampa Mountain trail for panoramic views over terracotta rooftops backed by forested mountains. Day two is a full day in Piatra Craiului National Park, beginning with the Zarnesti Gorge and ascending the dramatic limestone ridge. This is a serious mountain day with approximately 1,000m elevation gain, involving sections of easy scrambling (UIAA Grade I) with some fixed chains on the steepest pitches. Crucially, the ridge has two bailout points where marked trails descend back to the valley floor, so hikers caught by deteriorating weather are never fully committed. Day three offers a choice between the Postavaru Peak trail for sweeping Carpathian views or the Bucegi Plateau cable car option with hiking among dramatic wind-carved rock formations at 2,000m.

**Days 4-5: The Saxon Heartland -- History and Hospitality.** Day four travels to Viscri, a tiny village with a UNESCO-listed fortified church, and includes a half-day hike through surrounding pastoral hills and oak forests connecting scattered Saxon villages. What makes Viscri memorable is the quality of the experience: dinner at a village guesthouse means home-cooked Romanian food eaten often with the host family, accompanied by tuica (plum brandy) -- this is Transylvania's version of apres-hike culture. The day ends in Sighisoara, the birthplace of Vlad Tepes (the historical Dracula) and one of the best-preserved inhabited medieval citadels in all of Europe. Day five continues to Sibiu, a former European Capital of Culture with a stunning Germanic old town and the Brukenthal Museum (one of the oldest museums in Europe, founded 1790). The afternoon hike follows the Sub Cetate trail through the forested hills above the city.

**Days 6-7: Alpine Finale and Fortress Close.** Day six drives a section of the Transfagarasan Highway -- one of the world's most dramatic mountain roads -- to Balea Lake at 2,034 metres, with a hike up to the Balea ridgeline (4-6 hours, moderate-to-difficult). If the Transfagarasan is closed, the strong alternative is the Cindrel Mountains above Sibiu. Day seven begins with Rasnov Fortress, a 13th-century hilltop citadel built by Teutonic Knights, where the fortress trail and surrounding forest network offer a proper morning hike, followed by a return to Brasov for a final evening in the old town.

**Safety and Practical Considerations.** The itinerary passes through areas with significant brown bear populations, particularly around Piatra Craiului. Bear encounters are uncommon but a real possibility requiring behavioral mitigation: hike in groups, make consistent noise, avoid dawn and dusk, never leave food unsecured. Bear spray (available at outdoor shops in Brasov) is the recommended last-resort tool. On ridge trails, mobile phone signal is patchy; download offline maps before setting out. Rescue from exposed ridge sections could take several hours, making proactive bailout more important than pushing through deteriorating conditions. Romania's mountain rescue service (Salvamont) is experienced and professional. The nearest well-equipped hospitals are in Brasov and Sibiu, both visited during the trip. A rental car is strongly recommended; budget approximately 250-350 euros for a week including fuel, insurance, and supplementary coverage. Romanian mountain roads require attentive driving, particularly the Transfagarasan. Romania is a cash-heavier economy than Western Europe; carry lei in cash as ATMs may not exist in smaller villages.

Budget 750 to 1,150 euros per person for the full week including car rental with insurance, accommodation in guesthouses and pensions, meals, fuel, and entry fees. This is roughly half the price of comparable trips in Austria or Norway and a third the price of Switzerland, while offering hiking and historical richness that rivals any of them.

**Why it survived:** The Transylvania Circuit consistently outperformed competing ideas across the adversarial process, ultimately achieving a final aggregate score of 7.8 compared to 5.8 for Lofoten and 7.0 for Hadrian's Wall. Its primary strengths were Cost (9/10 in all rounds) and Challenge (7-8/10), where it demonstrated superior value and genuine personal growth through alpine scrambling, cultural immersion in an unfamiliar environment, and navigating a cash-heavy foreign-language context. While Safety remained its weakest dimension due to bear encounters and rescue response times, the final round itinerary addressed this gap far more thoroughly than previous versions, providing specific mitigation strategies, bailout routes, and honest risk context. Fun improved from 7 to 8 between rounds as the day-by-day pacing was refined to balance mountain days with cultural experiences and warm village hospitality. The combination of practical logistics guidance (cash economy, insurance details, driver-guide options) and detailed safety planning (bear spray sourcing, ridge bailout routes, Salvamont contact information) transformed it from a conceptually strong idea to an actionable itinerary. Lofoten was evicted early due to poor cost value (4/10) and minimal challenge (5/10) despite good safety; Hadrian's Wall also struggled on challenge (4/10 initially) and had limited fun (6/10) due to England's wet weather and modest landscapes. By the final round, the Transylvania Circuit had addressed its key critic concerns while competitors could not bridge their fundamental weaknesses.

## 3. Ideas Overview

3 ideas were generated in Round 1:

1. **Lofoten Islands, Norway -- Arctic Viking Trail Week** -- Explore the Arctic archipelago combining dramatic granite peaks, Viking history, and the midnight sun in guaranteed cool temperatures (12-17°C), with base-hopping between fishing villages, visits to the Lofotr Viking Museum, and stays in traditional rorbuer cabins.

2. **Transylvania Circuit -- Carpathian Peaks, Fortified Churches, and Medieval Citadels** -- Trace a loop through Romania combining alpine hiking in the Carpathians with medieval Saxon history, fortified churches, and UNESCO sites in a cost-effective, off-the-beaten-path destination.

3. **Walk the Roman Frontier -- Hadrian's Wall Path into the English Lake District** -- Combine two to four days walking along the 1,900-year-old Roman wall in northern England with active archaeological sites, then transition to Lake District fell hiking across UNESCO World Heritage terrain.
