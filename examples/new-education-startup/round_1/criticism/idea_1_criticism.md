## Evaluation: Apprentice Graph -- A Peer-to-Peer Skill Marketplace Where Learners Teach What They Just Learned

### Scores

| Dimension | Score (1-10) | Justification |
|---|---|---|
| Pedagogical Value | 6 | Near-peer tutoring has research support, but the platform provides no structured pedagogy, curriculum design, or quality control over what is actually taught. |
| Feasibility | 4 | The cold-start problem, quality assurance at scale, and knowledge graph construction present severe practical challenges that the idea hand-waves past. |
| Differentiation | 7 | The rotating tutor/learner model and credit economy genuinely distinguish this from existing tutoring marketplaces. |

### Critical Analysis

#### Pedagogical Value (Score: 6)
The idea correctly identifies the protege effect and near-peer tutoring as evidence-backed pedagogical strategies. However, the platform as described has no mechanism to ensure that what a near-peer tutor teaches is actually correct. Someone who "learned Python three months ago" and passed a basic assessment may have significant misconceptions that they confidently transmit to new learners. The claim that recent learners are "often better teachers" than experts is an oversimplification of the research -- studies on peer instruction typically occur in structured educational settings with instructor oversight, curated materials, and controlled environments, none of which exist here. The platform relies entirely on unstructured live human interaction with no mention of lesson plans, learning objectives, standardised content, or any scaffolding to guide tutors in what to cover. The "built-in assessments" are mentioned in passing but there is no detail on their rigour or how they prevent someone with shallow understanding from being surfaced as a tutor. The knowledge graph tracking "how effectively they teach" is appealing in theory, but the feedback signal is noisy -- a learner may rate a tutor highly because they were friendly, not because they learned anything durable. Without any mechanism to validate actual learning outcomes (pre/post assessments, retention testing, project-based verification), the pedagogical value is assumed rather than engineered.

#### Feasibility (Score: 4)
This idea faces multiple severe feasibility challenges that are either unacknowledged or dismissed too lightly. First, the cold-start problem is not solved by the claim that "every learner becomes a future tutor." At launch, there are zero tutors. You need a critical mass of people who have already learned skills and are willing to teach them before any learner gets value. The idea provides no concrete strategy for seeding the supply side. Second, the knowledge graph described -- tracking what users know, how well they teach specific topics, optimal concept sequencing, and matching across learning profiles -- is an enormously complex system to build. This is not a standard recommendation engine; it requires validated skill assessments, multi-dimensional teaching quality metrics, and enough interaction data to produce meaningful signals. Building this to the level of precision described ("a user who explains recursion brilliantly but struggles to teach database normalization gets routed accordingly") requires thousands of data points per user per skill, which will take years to accumulate. Third, the dual-currency credit economy introduces economic design complexity that is easy to describe and extremely hard to balance in practice -- if credits are too easy to earn, the paid tier has no value; if too hard, users churn. Fourth, the enterprise sales motion (bulk credit pools for corporate teams) requires an entirely different go-to-market from a consumer peer-to-peer platform, effectively doubling the business complexity. Fifth, scheduling and availability are unaddressed -- matching someone who wants to learn recursion right now with someone who recently learned it and is available to teach at that moment, in the same timezone, is a logistics problem that grows harder before it grows easier.

#### Differentiation (Score: 7)
The rotating role model is a genuinely novel structural difference from platforms like Preply, Wyzant, or Chegg Tutors, which all maintain a fixed tutor/student dichotomy. The credit economy that ties learning to teaching is creative and creates a distinctive value proposition. The knowledge graph aspiration, if realised, would be a genuine moat. However, the differentiation weakens on closer inspection: platforms like Brainly, StudyPool, and even informal communities on Discord already facilitate peer-to-peer learning, albeit without the formalised credit system. The enterprise positioning ("activate internal knowledge transfer") is not new -- knowledge management platforms like Degreed, Docebo, and even Microsoft Viva Learning already target internal skill sharing. The core differentiator is really the credit economy and the algorithmic near-peer matching, which are meaningful but narrower than the idea presents.

#### Engagement (Score: 5)
The credit economy provides a transactional incentive to teach, but this is extrinsic motivation that research consistently shows is weaker for sustained engagement than intrinsic motivation. The idea assumes people will want to teach after they have learned, but teaching is effortful and many learners will simply want to move on to their next learning goal -- the "skip teaching" paid tier implicitly acknowledges this, which undermines the self-sustaining economy claim. If a significant fraction of users pay to skip teaching, the supply side thins out and the credit economy collapses or requires heavy subsidy. On the learner side, engagement depends on tutor quality and availability being consistently good. One bad tutoring session -- a tutor who is unclear, unprepared, or simply not very good at teaching despite having passed an assessment -- will damage retention. The idea has no described mechanism for session structure, conversation guides, or quality floors. The "increasingly precise matching over time" is a future-state promise that does nothing for early users, who will experience the weakest version of the platform. There is also no mention of community features, gamification beyond credits, progress visualisation, or any of the engagement mechanics that successful edtech platforms use to keep users returning.
